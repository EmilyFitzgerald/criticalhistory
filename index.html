The Spirit Moves: A History of Black Social Dance on Film, 1900-1987

Chapter 2, Part 1: The Blues.

Contextual Information

Filmmaker: Maura Dehn (Russian American)

Year: 195? (TBC)

Dancers:

  • Rent Party: whole ensemble dancing (starts 0:58 Of video 1)
  • Shake Blues: Sandra Gibson (starts 0:02 of video 2)
  • Speak Easy: Leon James, Al Minns, and guest partner (starts 2:27 of video 2)
  • Male Shake Blues: Al Minns (starts 5:30 of video 2) Example in ballroom (starts 6:54 of video 2)
  • Gutbucket Blues: Sandra Gibson and James Berry (guest artist) video 3

Filming Location:

Source type: Documentary

In original form, the documentary is six hours long, including archival footage from the 50s to the 80s. Only a few institutions have the full film; first three parts (two hours long) released on DVD 2008 (ACMI has it in their collection; lots of clips of it on YouTube.

Dehn was Russian, trained as a ballet dancer. She saw Josephine Baker in Paris in the 1920s, performing Charleston. Moved to New York 1930, went to the Savoy Ballroom soon after, fell in love with jazz, immersed herself in the scene, wanted to preserve for future generations.

The documentary is split into three parts, each prefaced by Dehn, but then letting the footage speak for itself.

Part 1, turn of the century to 1950; Part 2 filmed at the savoy; Part 3 1950-80s, in a public school.

Dehn wanted to include dancing from the turn of the century, but of course there was no footage, so got Savoy Ballroom dancers to recreate the older styles in a studio; she then used the same format for the modern styles. A seperate soundtrack was recorded, due to technology available to her.

Clyde Maxwell Blues:

Contextual Information

Filmmaker: Alan Lomax (White American), John Bishop, and Worth Long

Year: September 3, 1978

Dancers: Bea Maxwell and Lilian Simpson (Clyde Jude Maxwell playing) Dancing starts at 0:55

Filming Location: Maxwell’s farm near Canton, Mississippi

Type: Archival footage

Alan Lomax was an American ethnomusicologist (1915-2002). ‘From 1978 to 1985, Lomax travelled throughout the South collecting recordings in five culture regions - Mississippi Delta and Hill Country, Central and Southern Appalachia, New Orleans, Cajun Louisiana and John Island, S.C. - compiling footage for what would become his “American Patchwork” series on PBS. (Info from ‘place stories’)

Alan Lomax audio archive available online at the Association for Cultural Equity: http://research.culturalequity.org/audio-guide.jsp; videos from Alan Lomax are also on their website, and on a YouTube channel https://m.youtube.com/user/AlanLomaxArchive

The Blood of Jesus, aka The Glory Road

Contextual Information

Filmmaker: Spencer Williams (African American)

Year: 1941

Dancers: Unknown (check IMDb credits?)

Filming Location: Unknown (movie studio, Dallas, Texas)

Type: Movie

This is a dance clip from a movie, The a Blood of Jesus, aka The Glory Road. Written and directed by Spencer Williams (African American); released 1941

The movie is set in the rural South - a ‘godly young woman’ wounded by her atheist husband; goes to the crossroads, gets tempted by an agent of the devil with nice clothes, the city, floor show includes an acrobat and a jazz singer; gets a job at a juke joint; has a moral crisis about if she has chosen the right path and ends up fleeing back to the crossroads. However she is mistaken for a pickpocket, is chased by a group of men. At the crossroads Satan is waiting for her with a jazz band on a flatbed truck. She is protected by the angel, who drives the mob away; crossroads becomes a crucified Christ. Martha wakes, healed, and is reunited with her husband, who has embraced religion.

Produced in Texas with a $5,000 budget, Screened in cinemas and black churches - commercial success, led to Williams being offered a ten year contract to produce eight more films with the company, including two more religious ones.

(Whole movie (57 minutes long) available on YouTube).
